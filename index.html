South American Political Advertisers Spent at Least US$357 Million on Facebook and Instagram

Presidents and parties’ verified accounts boosted their posts before elections; Meta’s data exposes which other organizations support the proselitist posts, while media outlets and different users registered promoted content too.

By Ignacio Grimaldi

An article written for the Lede Program at Columbia University

Posts about “social, electoral or political issues” can shape public opinion—and they come at a price. Over the past five years, presidents, candidates, political parties, government agencies, nongovernmental organizations and media outlets across South America spent at least US$357 million to reach larger audiences on Facebook and Instagram, according to records from Meta’s Ad Library.

The dataset covers 12 South American countries and includes more than 10 million paid advertisements related to social issues, elections and politics. Brazil accounted for most of those ads, but Ecuador’s president spent more than any other head of state in the region. The data also reveal organizations with no obvious political identity that spent money in this tool to promote partisan content.

The findings are based on records collected by Meta, which disclose how much advertisers spent to promote posts on Facebook and Instagram and identify the individuals or organizations behind those campaigns. Because Meta reports spending in local currencies, all figures were converted into U.S. dollars using each country’s official exchange rate.

Some of the biggest advertisers were the verified Facebook and Instagram accounts of Brazil’s Partido dos Trabalhadores (PT), the party of President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva; Argentina’s La Libertad Avanza, the party of President Javier Milei. Others included verified accounts belonging to political figures such as Chilean President José Antonio Kast and his Paraguayan colleague Santiago Peña, as well as pages promoting content in support of Bolivian politician Rodrigo Paz and Uruguay’s Yamandú Orsi Presidente campaign. Perú represents a special situation since there’s still no official decision about the last electoral result.

In every case, the analysis also includes the verified regional, departmental and provincial accounts of the political parties to which these figures belong. That explains the spending attributed to La Libertad Avanza. While the party’s main verified account spent US$209,231 on advertisements, its provincial accounts increased the total to US$739,994. Those advertisements were reinforced by other organizations aligned with the libertarian movement’s so-called “cultural battle,” amplifying President Milei’s message on social media. One example is the digital platform Ratio Oficial, whose advertisements totaled about US$840,000 in spending. Ratio Oficial displays the logo of Fundación Faro, an Argentina-based organization.

When the Money Was Spent

Advertising activity closely tracked the electoral calendar. Some campaigns created dedicated Facebook pages for presidential races, such as Brazil’s “Eleição 2022 Luiz Lula da Silva Presidente” and Uruguay’s “Yamandú Orsi Presidente”. Others relied on existing accounts but sharply increased their advertising in the weeks leading up to Election Day.

That was the case for Peru’s Fuerza Popular, led by Keiko Fujimori. During the month before this year’s two presidential elections, the party concentrated 87% of its total promoted content. More than half of Ecuadorian President Daniel Noboa’s advertisements also were activated during the 30 days before his country’s election.

The most successful sponsored posts also tended to appear during that pre-election window. For example, Noboa promoted content between Oct. 22 and Oct. 29 last year, roughly three weeks before a national referendum. The post generated more than one million impressions and cost between US$25,000 and US$30,000 in four ads, according to Meta’s records.

A similar pattern emerged in Argentina. Ahead of the country’s 2025 midterm elections, the most-viewed sponsored post from the page Alianza La Libertad Avanza PBA—a reference to Buenos Aires Province, Argentina’s largest electoral district—ran two weeks before Election Day. The post surpassed one million impressions and, according to Meta, cost at least US$687 to promote. “I have no doubt that together we will make Argentina great again,” Milei said in the sponsored video, echoing a slogan associated with U.S. President Donald Trump’s MAGA movement. Also, it shows a Boca Juniors’ shirt, which a popular football team.

Presidents and political parties are not the only major advertisers on Meta’s platforms. Looking only at the top 10 advertisers in each of the 12 countries, politicians and government accounts represented 78.2% of the group. The remainder included media organizations, nongovernmental organizations and other entities.

In Argentina, the government of Córdoba Province was the country’s largest advertiser. In Bolivia, the government of Santa Cruz ranked third.

Among nongovernmental organizations, Greenpeace Brazil ranked eighth nationwide in advertising expenditures and also appeared among the largest advertisers in Chile.

Overall, Meta’s records show 10,026,593 sponsored posts related to what the company classifies as “social issues, elections or politics.” More than half originated in Brazil.

Brazilian advertisers accounted for 68.4% of all such advertisements—more than five times Argentina’s share of 11.7%, the second-highest in the region. Together, the two countries generated roughly four out of every five sponsored political posts.

Although more Brazilian pages paid to promote content, Argentine pages were more active on average. Argentine advertisers ran an average of 22 advertisements per page, compared with 19 among Brazilian advertisers.

Meta’s database reveals who paid to promote a post and how much they spent. But it leaves one fundamental question unanswered: Where did the money come from?
